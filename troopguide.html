A GUIDE TO REALISTIC TROOPS (14TH–15TH CENTURY)

« Not every man in armor is a knight. Not every sword is meant for war. »

This guide helps players design believable armies, garrisons, and retinues grounded in late-medieval reality. Use it to structure troops, equipment, and ranks in a way that makes your world feel alive and coherent.

2. Key Definitions

Knights

  • Landed nobles or vassals; symbols of chivalry and authority.
  • Fight on horseback but often dismount by this era.
  • Full plate or brigandine over mail, lance and sword, destrier or courser.
  • Addressed as “Ser.” Only a few per noble house (2–5 typical).

Men-at-Arms

  • Paid professionals or retainers sworn to a lord.
  • Former squires, lesser knights, or mercenaries.
  • Equipped with halberds, poleaxes, or warhammers; brigandine or half-plate.
  • The backbone of every serious retinue.

Footmen / Guards

  • Permanent retainers of a keep or town; maintain order and defense.
  • Padded jack or short mail, kettle helm, spear or bill.
  • Distinct from levies — they are paid and drilled regularly.
  • In RP: a manor or town might have 8–20 footmen total.

Levies

  • Peasants raised temporarily for defense or war.
  • Little or no training; minimal armor, farm tools adapted for battle.
  • Return to their fields once the war ends.
  • Never use levies as daily guards — they are seasonal troops.

Archers & Crossbowmen

  • Skilled commoners trained from youth.
  • Longbowmen — disciplined and deadly at range.
  • Crossbowmen — easier to train, slower to reload.
  • Light armor; travel with pavises or stakes for cover.

Clarification — Longbowmen: Though common-born, longbowmen were elite specialists. In training and battlefield value, they stood between skilled commoners and professionals. In social rank, however, they remained yeomen, not knights. Place them at Tier III, but treat elite longbow companies as near-professional formations.

4. Common Mistakes in RP

  • ❌ Too many knights. Knighthood was rare and symbolic.
  • ❌ Over-armored peasants. Mail and plate were costly; commoners wore padded jacks.
  • ❌ Mixing guards and soldiers. Guards keep order; soldiers fight wars.
  • ❌ Standing armies everywhere. Only major realms or orders maintained permanent forces.

6. Historical Parallels: Real Noble Retinues (1300–1450)

To help gauge realistic troop numbers for noble houses, here are examples of actual medieval lords and their recorded musters.

Sir John Fastolf of Caister (England, c. 1430)

The archetype of a professional late-medieval retainer lord.
  • Status: Wealthy knight and veteran of the Hundred Years’ War.
  • Lands: Several manors in Norfolk.
  • Standing Retinue: ~20–25 men-at-arms, 80–100 archers in permanent pay.
  • Full Muster: Could raise ~200 when calling all household retainers and tenants.
  • Notes: Famous for his detailed “indentures of service.” His household was closer to a baronial retinue.

Baron Talbot (Shrewsbury, 1417)

Fought under Henry V during the French campaigns.
  • Status: English baron commanding under royal banner.
  • Muster Roll at Caen (1417): 6 knights, 44 men-at-arms, 120 archers.
  • Total: ~170 troops.
  • Notes: Entirely personal retinue, not including allies or levies.

Duke of Burgundy (Philip the Good, 1440s)

One of the strongest feudal hosts of the 15th century.
  • Permanent Standing Force (“Compagnies d’Ordonnance”): 1,200 men-at-arms, 600 mounted archers, 1,000 foot archers — ~3,000 total.
  • Levy Capacity: Could raise 15,000–20,000 including vassals and hired men.
  • Wealth: Comparable to a small kingdom.

Sir Thomas Erpingham (Agincourt, 1415)

Commander of the English archers at Agincourt.
  • Household Strength: ~80–100 men (mostly archers).
  • Role: Retinue captain and field commander for royal service.
  • Notes: Represents the well-connected knightly household with professional retainers.

Lord de Clifford (England, 1380)

A Marcher Lord defending the Scottish border.
  • Border Muster (Wardrobe Accounts): 5 knights, 47 men-at-arms, 128 archers.
  • Total: ~180 men.
  • Notes: A defensive regional lord with castle garrisons and rotating levies.

Minor Manor Example

Average gentry or landed knight.
  • Typical muster: 1–2 men-at-arms (family + squire), 3–5 archers or servants-at-arms.
  • Total: 5–10 men.
  • Notes: Many “houses” in RP are actually this size. These lords serve as vassals to larger counts or barons.

Interpretation for RP

  • Personal Retinue: Even great lords rarely had more than 200–300 men permanently in pay.
  • Feudal Muster: Counts and dukes could call up 1000–2000 by summoning vassals and town militias.
  • Temporary Nature: Once a campaign ended, levies and vassal troops returned home.
  • Command Hierarchy: Each vassal led his own contingent under the count’s banner, forming the full host.

Example — Aldenwall: Count William Davenmoore could command roughly 150–200 professional retainers and knights, but when his vassal houses answer the call, his host swells to nearly 1000 men. This mirrors the structure of a 15th-century comital army and fits perfectly within historical ratios.

In short, a thousand men is the strength of a powerful but not royal house — a realistic upper limit for any count or regional lord in a low-fantasy setting.

“A lord’s strength lies not in numbers, but in the quality of the few who stand when others flee.”
