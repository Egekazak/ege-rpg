A FIELD GUIDE TO LOW-FANTASY WARFARE

« This guide is meant for storytellers, writers, and roleplayers who want their wars to feel real — not as endless duels of demigods, but as the cold, muddy grind of men and women trying to survive the march, the rain, and the next ambush. »

Most lords and armies did everything they could to avoid a real battle — and with good reason. Between the big set-piece battles of the age, 99% of the fighting was a constant grind of skirmishes, chevauchées, ambushes, raids, and siege actions — the true daily bread of 14th–15th century war.

Most medieval soldiers lived and died in these skirmishes.

What follows is a breakdown of how those skirmishes actually looked, felt, and functioned — the rhythm of war that defined an entire age.

What “Skirmish” Really Meant (1300–1450)

When chroniclers say “they met in a skirmish” (conflixerunt in praelio modico, ein kleines Treffen), it could mean anything from 50 to 1,000 men.

You can picture it: scouts and foragers bumping into each other out on the road, a few shouted orders, arrows flying — not the tidy battle lines paintings love to show.

A typical skirmish looked like this:

  1. Foraging or raiding force (maybe 100–500 men) rides out to burn villages or capture supplies.
  2. Enemy scouts spot them.
  3. A response party (local knights, sergeants, or militia) intercepts them.
  4. A few hours of chaotic mounted combat, dismounts, arrow volleys, and pursuit follow.
  5. Both sides disengage when wounded or captives pile up — before things spiral into disaster.

These fights might last minutes to an hour, with casualties often under a hundred — but reputations were made and lost in them.

“A skirmish can cost a captain his company, or make his name feared for years.”

Skirmish Archetypes

1. Ambush in terrain

  • Dense forest, ravine, or narrow road.
  • Scouts or vanguard trapped by concealed archers or crossbowmen.
  • Short, violent, over within half an hour.
    Example: English longbow ambushes in Normandy, or Hussite wagon traps in Bohemia.

2. Chevauchée interception

  • One side raids (burning villages, taking livestock).
  • The other shadows them, trying to force a fight at a bridge or ford.
  • Archers and light horse exchange missiles, then cavalry charge.
    Example: Hundred Years’ War — the Black Prince’s raids through France, harassed constantly by French companies.

3. Castle harassment

  • Defenders sally out when besiegers get careless.
  • Short cavalry sortie to destroy siege engines or kill engineers.
  • The besiegers rally, push them back inside.
    Example: Standard around every 14th c. fortress siege; endlessly repeated.

4. Pitched patrol clash

  • Both sides’ vanguards meet by accident while scouting.
  • Neither wants a full battle; both deploy small tactical formations.
  • Archers and crossbows open; cavalry probe flanks; then withdrawal.
    Example: On the eve of big battles like Poitiers or Agincourt, these encounters decided where and when the real fight would occur.

How It Looked in Action

“Here’s how such a meeting of arms might unfold — a slice of war as it truly was.”

Imagine a small plain between two villages in 1405:

  • 50 mounted men-at-arms, 80 crossbowmen, and 20 light horse ride out under a banner.
  • Opposing them, maybe 60 mounted men, mostly local retainers and hired riders.
  • Both deploy quickly — no grand speeches, no formations.
  • Crossbow volleys crack; horses scream; a few riders charge and collide.
  • Once one side’s captain is unhorsed or captured, morale breaks, and the rest scatter.
  • Survivors flee into woods or back to camp.

Casualties: perhaps 10 dead, 20 wounded, a handful captured for ransom.
Result: both sides claim victory in their chronicles.

Why Skirmishes Mattered More Than Battles

FactorReason
EconomyArmies fought to destroy harvests and ransack supply lines, not annihilate each other.
Ransom cultureCapturing nobles for money was more profitable than killing them.
Psychological warfareBurning and raiding forced opponents to react or pay tribute.
AttritionSkirmishes bled manpower and morale slowly without risking the whole army.
PoliticsLords preferred limited engagements — a lost skirmish wasn’t a catastrophe, a lost battle was.

A skirmish was less about “winning” and more about wearing down, delaying, and learning — who’s camped where, how fast they can mobilize, which captain is bold or timid.

The Nature of War

“Battles are rare. War is hunger, smoke, and rumor.”

Combat Philosophy

“Victory means living to burn their harvest next month.”
  • Avoid risk. Commanders withdraw before full losses.
  • Capture over kill. Nobles worth ransom; commoners less so.
  • Morale is everything. Noise, fear, smoke, and weather break armies before blades do.
  • Magic ≠ artillery. Treat magic as force multipliers, not battlefield-ending nukes.
  • Survival first. Retreat, regroup, ransom, revenge — the cycle of war.

Magic & Myth in Realism

Use magic sparingly and consistently, as if it were another military tool.

SchoolGrounded Military Role
AlchemyGunpowder analogues: firepots, smoke, healing tonics.
NecromancyCheap labor and scouts; stigmatized but effective.
DivinationReconnaissance and morale (omens, blessings).
IllusionCamouflage, confusion, or deception in fog and smoke.
Runic EnchantmentsIncremental improvements: longer blade life, steadier aim.
“If magic replaces skill, it cheapens victory. If it enhances skill, it elevates heroes.”

Narrating Skirmishes

Tips for DMs or storytellers:

  1. Start in motion. Players join mid-march or mid-ambush.
  2. Keep the scale intimate. Emphasize confusion, noise, dust, shouting.
  3. Describe environment, not just weapons. “The smell of trampled grass and sweat outweighs the sound of swords.”
  4. Reward tactical thinking. Terrain, timing, morale, fatigue.
  5. Make retreat honorable. The goal is survival, not glory.

The Battle of Grunwald (1410)

“When banners met that day, the world held its breath.”

While most of medieval war was fought in raids and sieges, there were moments when everything came to a head — when entire realms gambled their armies in a single day. The Battle of Grunwald, also known as Tannenberg, was one of those moments.

The Combatants

In July of 1410, the Teutonic Order, a crusading state of armored knights and mercenaries, faced the united forces of Poland and Lithuania under King Władysław II Jagiełło and Grand Duke Vytautas.

It was a war born of ambition, trade, and faith — the Order’s last attempt to dominate the Baltic and subdue its neighbors under the banner of a “Northern Crusade.”

Each side brought tens of thousands to the field: knights, men-at-arms, footmen, and banners from nearly every corner of Central Europe. Contemporary estimates speak of 50,000–60,000 soldiers, making it one of the largest battles of the Middle Ages north of the Alps.

Why It Was Exceptional

FactorWhy It Stands Out
ScaleMost medieval armies numbered in the hundreds or low thousands. Grunwald was tens of thousands.
DocumentationChronicles, payrolls, and letters survive — we know the names of mercenary captains and even how many men they led.
DiversityGermans, Poles, Lithuanians, Czechs, Hungarians, Tatars — a cross-section of Europe.
DurationThe battle raged for nearly half a day — unusually long for pre-gunpowder warfare.
DestructionThe Teutonic Order lost nearly its entire leadership. Grand Master Ulrich von Jungingen was killed; hundreds of knights captured or slain.
AftermathThe Order’s defeat marked the decline of the crusading ideal in Europe — the last time holy war and feudal ambition met on such a scale.

The Cost of Glory

Contemporary witnesses wrote of a field so littered with bodies that horses could not ride through. The sun baked the fallen in their armor; scavengers and carrion birds covered the plain for days. The victors, though triumphant, were too exhausted to press their advantage — their “victory” bled them dry.

“One day of glory can cost a generation of strength.”

The following months brought plague, famine, and bankrupt treasuries as ransoms and burials consumed the survivors’ spoils.

A Lesson for Storytellers

Grunwald shows what happens when all restraint breaks — when every noble, mercenary, and zealot throws himself into a single gamble. It is the kind of battle that ends eras, not just campaigns. And it is the reason most commanders, both real and fictional, avoid battle whenever possible.

A full-scale clash like this is the exception — a storm that changes kingdoms, remembered for centuries precisely because no one dared repeat it.

Use in Roleplay

  • Treat it as a historical event, not a session encounter.
  • Let the aftermath — famine, ransom, trauma, legend — shape the world far more than the clash itself.
  • Make your players feel how rare and terrifying it is to see thousands on a single field, knowing one poor decision could doom a realm.
